# The Remarkable Rise of Greenhushing: Companies Quietly Pursue Climate Goals

Businesses that once trumpeted climate goals are now quietly working toward them, a phenomenon dubbed greenhushing. Corporate sustainability officers report increasing pressure to avoid public commitments that might attract political backlash or accusations of greenwashing. Despite the lower profile, many firms continue to invest in emissions reduction, supply chain decarbonization, and renewable energy procurement. The shift reflects a maturing of corporate climate strategy from marketing to operations. Science-based targets have become standard practice among Fortune 500 companies, with third-party verification ensuring accountability. Carbon accounting methodologies have improved significantly, allowing more precise tracking of scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions. The voluntary carbon market has faced credibility challenges but is evolving toward higher-quality offsets. Investor pressure through initiatives like Climate Action 100+ continues to drive corporate decarbonization. The Inflation Reduction Act and EU Green Deal have created powerful incentives for clean technology investment. Some critics argue that greenhushing reduces transparency and public accountability, potentially slowing the broader energy transition.
